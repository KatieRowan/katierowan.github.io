Product Showcase Website

My Roles: Information Architecture, Testing, Branding, Visual Design

I designed a website to market PlanIt,a mobile app that helps students organize their homework and schoolwork. This site highlights key features of the app, crafts a sense of PlanIt's brand identity, and encourages visitors to download.

01
Background

A Quick Summary of PlanIt

The mobile app PlanIt tackles the challenge of balancing nightly asssignments with long-term projects, ensuring that students never miss a deadline. Over the course of a few weeks, I ran PlanIt's design and development from initial brainstorming to clickable prototype. After the prototype was finished, I embarked on designing a product showcase website that would help to market PlanIt.

If you'd like to know more about the process of designing the app PlanIt, view the full case study.

02
Research

Reflecting on Student Surveys

Through my work on PlanIt, I spoke with a number of students ranging from 4th graders to adult learners. I gained some valuable qualitative feedback through my initial surveys that proved helpful in my design of PlanIt's website.

What do you like most about the way you keep track of homework?

"Simple and clean interface."

"Ability to visualize on a calendar."

"It’s easy to see at a glance what my nightly homework is."

What do you like least about the way you keep track of homework?

"If I forget the planner then I don't know what I have to do."

"If I don’t have my agenda or if I forget to record my assignments I’m screwed!"

"Not homework specific."

Eyeing The Competition

I had already performed a full competitive analysis on three competitors (Google Calendar, Wunderlist, and myHomework). I took a look at each app's companion website to compare their marketing strategies and design features.

Opportunities:

- Highlighting that PlanIt was designed with homework in mind

- Creating a website that appeals to kids and adults alike

- Providing clear information on PlanIt's simple interface

Obstacles:

- Crafting name/brand recognition

- The market is saturated with general to-do list apps

- Appealing to older users

Identifying The Users

Through PlanIt's initial research, I identified three key demographics that I thought about while designing the app and made user personas. With the product showcase website, I wanted to keep these users in mind.

The Typical Student: Meg, an 8th grade student. Key takeaway for the website: highlight the features that Meg wants, but remember a parent/guardian will potentially be downloading the app.

The Graduate Student: Carrie, a 27-year-old woman who is working on her Master's Degree. Key takeaway for the website: illustrate how PlanIt is useful for all ages, not just for young students.

The Teacher: Craig, a 26-year-old high school science teacher. Key takeaway for the website: make sure to show how PlanIt teaches students executive functioning and independence.

03
Architecture

Going with the Flow

I wrote user stories to identify the main needs of a new user and a returning user. From these, I drafted the user flows that would occur on the showcase website. Building off the flows, I mapped out the needed sections of the site. As it was going to be a one-page landing page, I used Google Slides to create a sitemap that would help simulate the progression from section to section.

Back to Basics

I started out with quick 4-up sketches to get some general ideas for the website down on paper. This helped me iterate quickly without getting too attached to certain features and layouts.

From Sketches to Wireframes

From the best of my 4-up sketches, I created a high-fidelity wireframe for the website using Sketch that I could use for some preliminary testing.

Testing, Testing...

I conducted ten in-person walkthroughs with my high-fidelity wireframe version of the website. These rounds of testing provided me a few key points upon which to iterate.

Problem #1:
The layout was too repetitive. Multiple testers asked about the images accompanying the text throughout the body of the website. We talked through how it would look if different screenshots of the app were presented on mobile phone mockups. One tester summed up the general consensus: "This layout seems kind of boring."

Iteration #1:
I broke the monotony of the layout by playing around with the rhythm of elements on the page (while still adhering to a 12-column grid). Key highlights spanned the full width of the page, while other features were singled out in snapshots with text.

Problem #2:
The download button didn't match best practices. I had a single download button in the header of the website. This confused testers, who wondered how the app would be downloaded for a non-Apple device.

Iteration #2:
I did some research on Google Play and the App Store, and realized the best practice was to use the pre-designed download buttons provided for each platform. I integrated these into the mockups for ease of use.

04
Branding

For the product showcase app, I knew I wanted to incorporate PlanIt's branding while still allowing the screenshots and app highlights to shine. I used PlanIt's color palette and themes as a jumping off point (see PlanIt's case study for the full branding process). For the showcase, I introduced some new elements to make the website more interesting.

Illustrations

In order to create visual interest, I designed some custom illustrations that were used to highlight key features and break the rhythm of the page. I used PlanIt's pink highlight color in this linework to make these playful illustrations pop.

Space

As PlanIt's name emphasized outerspace, I wanted to incorporate space themes across the showcase. I did this in two ways: (1) ensuring plenty of vertical whitespace between elements on the page and (2) introducing a subtle starry background that became more pronounced as the user explored down the page.

06
Takeaways

1. Use standard call-to-action items for downloads.

Through testing, I found that users were confused by a download button that didn't match what they usually saw via Google Play and the App Store. I took the time to research best practices and implemented Google and Apple's download assets into the showcase.

2. Break up the rhythm to increase visual interest.

In my wireframes, I had too much of the same thing (screenshot, description, screenshot, description, screenshot...well, you get the idea). After discussing with testers, I found that I could keep their attention by changing up the rhythm on the page. This also helped with visual hierarchy -- highlights across the top of the site took up less space than key features that were explained further down the page.

3. Branding for a product showcase allows room for creativity.

When I first began to apply PlanIt's styles to the product showcase, I found that there was room for improvement. I was able to incorporate a fun starry background and custom illustrations that emphasized the themes of the app while still allowing the screenshots to shine.
